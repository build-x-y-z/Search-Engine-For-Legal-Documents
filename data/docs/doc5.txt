Privacy and Data Protection

Privacy law regulates the collection, use, disclosure, and protection of personal information. With the advent of digital technologies, data protection has become a critical area of legal compliance. Laws such as the European Unions General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) impose rigorous requirements on organizations that process personal data of EU residents, including principles of lawfulness, fairness, transparency, data minimization, purpose limitation, accuracy, storage limitation, integrity, and confidentiality. GDPR grants data subjects rights such as access, rectification, erasure (the "right to be forgotten"), restriction of processing, data portability, and the right to object to processing.

In the United States, data protection is governed by a patchwork of federal and state statutes. The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) protects health information, while the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (GLBA) safeguards financial data. The Childrens Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA) regulates the online collection of information from children under thirteen. State laws like the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) and the California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA) provide consumers with rights regarding the personal information held by businesses, including the right to know, delete, and opt out of the sale of personal data.

Organizations must implement appropriate technical and organizational measures to ensure a level of security appropriate to the risk. Data breach notification laws require entities to report security incidents to affected individuals and, in some cases, to regulators within a specified timeframe. Failure to comply with privacy laws can result in substantial fines, reputational damage, and litigation, including class actions.

Privacy considerations also intersect with employment, marketing, and surveillance. Employers may collect employee data for payroll and benefits but must respect employee privacy and comply with applicable laws. Marketing practices such as email campaigns and behavioral advertising must adhere to anti-spam laws like the CAN-SPAM Act and to privacy policies communicated to consumers.

Cross-border data transfers raise complex issues. Under GDPR, personal data may be transferred to countries with adequate protections as determined by the European Commission, or through mechanisms such as Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) and Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs). Recent legal developments, including the Schrems II decision, have affected the validity of data transfer frameworks and increased scrutiny on third-country recipients.

Data protection impact assessments (DPIAs) help organizations identify and mitigate risks associated with high-risk processing activities. Privacy by design and by default are key concepts requiring that privacy considerations be integrated into the development of products and services from inception.

Consent is a cornerstone of many data protection regimes, but it must be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous. Organizations should also consider legitimate interests as a lawful basis for processing, balancing their interests against the rights and freedoms of data subjects.

Privacy governance involves adopting policies, training employees, appointing data protection officers (where required), conducting audits, and maintaining records of processing activities. Incident response plans and vendor management are also critical aspects of compliance.

Because privacy laws continue to evolve globally, multinational organizations need to monitor legislative proposals, court decisions, and regulatory guidance. Engaging legal counsel with expertise in data protection helps ensure that data practices align with legal obligations while supporting business objectives.
